# Part V: The Future

## A Machine From 2026 Arrives in 1981

Imagine placing a modern smartphone on a desk in 1981.

At first, the object itself would seem miraculous. It is a camera without film, a library that fits in a hand, a music player, map, calculator, television studio and computer more powerful than machines occupying entire rooms.

Then the people examining it would discover something important. Much of the magic would not work.

There would be no modern cellular network, no public internet, no Wi-Fi, no cloud services, no app stores and no complete satellite system ready to guide the map. The phone would still be extraordinary, but the world that made it transformative had not yet been built.

To show someone in 1981 the real meaning of 2026 computing, we would have to bring much more than a device. We would need the networks, data centres, fibre-optic cables, satellites, software platforms, payment systems, digital records and billions of other connected machines. The revolution was never contained inside the computer. It came from the system that grew around it—and from the collapse in the cost of computation, storage and communication.

We may be making the same mistake when we look at AI today. We stare at the model and ask how intelligent it has become. The larger revolution will come from the world built around that intelligence: memory, agents, tools, tests, organisational knowledge, robots, processors, electricity and permission to act.

The model is the astonishing object on the desk. The future is the system that allows its capability to reach everything else.

Part V asks what happens as that surrounding system is built: which abilities become scarce when code becomes cheap, how the work of programmers changes, how organisations connect models to their own knowledge, and where these movements could lead in five and ten years. We begin with a basic economic rule: when one important cost falls, the bottleneck moves.
